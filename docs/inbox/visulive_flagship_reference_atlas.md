# VisuLive Flagship Reference Atlas

Date: 2026-04-08  
Status: North-star reference pack for lead agent + subagents

## What this pack is

This is not a moodboard dump.

It is a **curated reference atlas** for building VisuLive into a flagship visualizer / visual instrument / living reactive show system.

Use it to:
- sharpen taste
- prevent random effect accumulation
- give Codex stronger creative rails
- split reference material into actionable principles
- keep the team aimed at **jaw-dropping, room-commanding, authored spectacle**

Use this pack with:
- `docs/current-program.md`
- `docs/project-status.md`
- `docs/show-language.md`
- `docs/reference-systems.md`
- `docs/vision-ledger.md`

## Non-negotiable target

VisuLive is not trying to become a tasteful orb visualizer.

VisuLive is trying to become:
- a flagship big-screen visualizer
- a visual instrument
- a living stage entity
- a reactive performance system with consequence
- a screen-filling machine that can brood, gather, ignite, rupture, stain, haunt, and recover

The audience should feel:
- scale
- consequence
- buildup
- release
- aftermath
- memory
- authorship
- danger without chaos
- beauty without politeness

The final test is not “is this pretty?”

The final test is:
**would this feel incredible on a TV at a party, on a big LED wall, or in a dark room with music loud enough to matter?**

## Current-image read from the latest screenshots

From the current screenshots in this review set:
- the darkness is better than earlier soft-wash passes
- the cyan/electric core direction is promising

That means the next leap is **not** just hero polishing.

- stronger environmental participation

- stronger event cueing

- more authored dramaturgy above raw reactivity

## Master principle clusters

All references in this pack map back to one of these principle clusters.

### 1. Cue and dramaturgy
The visual must behave like a show with cues, holds, transitions, aftermath, and recoveries.

### 2. Compositing and screen-space consequence
The show must not rely only on 3D object lighting. It needs image-space event spending.

### 3. Signal hierarchy
Raw FFT is not enough. The system needs reduced, meaningful control signals and timing states.

### 4. Multi-layer stage image
The hero cannot carry the whole image forever. World, chamber, residue, architecture, and accent layers must all matter.

### 5. Dark-value discipline
Brightness is not the same as impact. Blacks must stay valuable so emitted events feel hot.

### 6. Memory and residue
Big moments must leave traces. The show must remember what just happened.

### 7. Flagship pacing
Not everything can be “on.” The show needs restraint, confidence, escalation, and clean resets.

# Reference families

## A. Show cue logic and playback grammar

These references matter because world-class shows are not just reactive. They are **organized in playable, timed, layered events**.

### grandMA3
Why it matters:
- professional lighting shows are organized around **cues, sequences, executors, timing, and phasers**, not one giant bag of parameters
- cues live inside sequences, executors control playback, and cue timing can be authored at multiple levels
- phasers provide rhythmic movement with speed, phase, width, transition, accel, and decel vocabulary

What to steal:
- a real VisuLive cue engine
- named cue classes instead of anonymous parameter spikes
- cue timing hierarchy
- phase offset thinking
- executor thinking for internal show layers
- “stomp / hold / resume / sync” style control grammar

What not to steal:
- console UX literally
- operator complexity for its own sake

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Build a layer above conductor detection that chooses from cue classes like:
- inhale
- tighten
- orbit widen
- halo ignite
- chamber sweep
- laser fan burst
- collapse
- aftermath stain
- ghost residue
- blackout hold
- cathedral reveal

### Pangolin BEYOND
Why it matters:
- laser shows are not generic glows; they are **precise geometric event machines**
- BEYOND is strong on live control, cue/group/master logic, multimedia timeline thinking, and color tuning

What to steal:
- laser-specific image grammar
- cue layering
- geometric discipline
- position/rotation/size/color as deliberate instruments
- the idea that laser output is an authored instrument, not an ambient decoration

What not to steal:
- sterile corporate laser-demo look
- safety-constraint aesthetics as the core identity

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Make the chamber capable of:
- fan sweeps
- line planes
- tunnel frames
- radial ignition
- slice planes
- scanning arcs
- tracked geometry bands

And make those feel like **show actions**, not background lines.

### Disguise
Why it matters:
- high-end show systems think in **layers, compositing, masks, blend modes, effect passes, and mapping**
- the visual result is often a composition problem as much as a scene problem

What to steal:
- compositing vocabulary
- mask-based event isolation
- additive, alpha, screen, overlay logic with intention
- effect-layer thinking

What not to steal:
- overproduced AV-demo polish with no soul

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Add a screen-space compositor vocabulary for:
- additive residue
- masked bloom zones
- layer-isolated chroma events
- event-localized flare and afterimage
- luma-based stains and fades

## B. Audio-reactive signal and timing systems

These references matter because the missing leap is not “more FFT.” It is **better reduction and better timing behavior**.

### Notch
Why it matters:
- Notch treats audio reactivity as a layered workflow and explicitly separates internal playback convenience from real live production posture
- it encourages broad musical envelopes, captured feeds, and precision audio-reactive nodes inside a post-heavy visual system

What to steal:
- multi-signal audio design
- live-production seriousness
- precision trigger + slow envelope coexistence
- post-heavy event design

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Stop thinking in “the audio drives the scene.”
Think in:
- tone pressure
- kick authority
- snare/tick accent
- shimmer density
- phrase confidence
- drop readiness
- aftermath decay

### TouchDesigner
Why it matters:
- TouchDesigner gives a clean mental model for **analysis operators + timed processes**
- Audio Spectrum CHOP exists specifically to calculate the frequency spectrum of the input signal
- Timer CHOP is explicitly an engine for timed processes, cues, playlists, state machines, and event callbacks

What to steal:
- explicit reduction pipelines
- timed-process engines
- cue scheduling
- segment thinking
- combining fast and slow signals intentionally

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Build or sharpen internal signal channels like:
- low-end pressure
- kick gate
- snare crack
- high-band spray
- vocal / presence window
- tension accumulator
- phrase turn detector
- decay memory

Then use a timer/state engine above them so the show can:
- gather before impact
- hold impact
- decay in authored stages
- deny over-triggering

### Resolume Wire
Why it matters:
- it offers FFT data as a 1024-float array and sits closer to actual VJ/live-show musical thinking than generic browser demos

What to steal:
- mapping specific bands to different consequence layers
- BPM / bar / phrase awareness
- patch-graph mentality for event routing

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Make sure low, mid, and high band families can affect:
- different chamber systems
- different accent systems
- different screen-space consequences

Not every band family should hit the hero equally.

## C. Shader-native and browser-native visual intensity

These references matter because VisuLive is browser-based and should learn from systems that already prove the web can go much harder.

### Synesthesia
Why it matters:
- SSF scenes are fundamentally fragment-shader based and add audio uniforms, controls, multiple passes, JavaScript per-frame scripting, and image inputs around that shader core
- the `script.js` step exists specifically because shader-only work lacks frame-wide memory and scene-wide calculation

What to steal:
- shader-first boldness
- multi-pass thinking
- frame memory workarounds
- pre-shader scripting for show logic
- image/textural support when useful

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Where the visual wants impossible-seeming luminous or hallucinated image behavior, do not assume it must all come from mesh/material logic.
A hybrid architecture is allowed:
- 3D hero/chamber base
- shader/post/event composite on top
- frame-history passes
- per-frame JS logic feeding shader uniforms

### projectM
Why it matters:
- projectM is still a mature reference for preset archetypes, strong transitions, audio analysis, and a huge vocabulary of visual identities

What to steal:
- archetype diversity
- hard contrast between modes
- the courage to transition between distinct identities
- the lesson that a convincing show system needs more than “same scene but louder”

What not to steal:
- preset soup
- random style roulette
- visual spam with no hierarchy

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Acts must differ more clearly.
Not just palette shifts.
Not just speed changes.
Actual identity differences.

### Butterchurn
Why it matters:
- it proves that **browser-native** visuals can still reach real intensity and preset-style transformation
- its preset blending language is useful as proof that transitions matter almost as much as the looks themselves

What to steal:
- browser confidence
- blend-transition thinking
- willingness to go harder in color and motion when the music earns it

What not to steal:
- constant rainbow soup
- “the coolest frame wins” instead of authored continuity

## D. Render/VFX architecture references

These references matter because VisuLive now needs stronger internal architecture, not just nicer surfaces.

### three.js WebGPU + TSL + node post stack
Why it matters:
- `WebGPURenderer` uses WebGPU by default with a WebGL2 fallback
- TSL includes post-processing utilities like `afterImage`, `anamorphic`, `bloom`, `chromaticAberration`, and `dof`
- WebGPU post nodes and pass nodes support more modern post composition and MRT-oriented workflows

What to steal:
- screen-space effect chains as first-class citizens
- selective / staged post
- MRT-aware render thinking
- afterimage and flare as authored tools, not hacks

Direct implication for VisuLive:
The next show pass should include a real post vocabulary such as:
- event bloom
- selective threshold bloom
- damped afterimage / trails
- chromatic stress on impact moments only
- anamorphic flare on beam / ring / laser events
- depth or mask aware haze / residue

### Unreal Niagara
Why it matters:
- Niagara organizes effects through **systems, emitters, modules, and parameters**
- newer lightweight/stateless emitters exist specifically to reduce cost and speed up iteration

What to steal:
- a modular VFX mental model
- separate effect families
- parameterized emitters with shared orchestration
- stateful vs stateless thinking

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Split the monolith into families like:
- hero shell emitter system
- chamber beam system
- halo/ring system
- residue/stain system
- accent/shard system
- volumetric dust / field system

Each family should own its own motion and rendering logic.

### vvvv gamma + RenderDoc / profiler culture
Why it matters:
- vvvv’s GPU debugging guidance and profiler culture reinforce that large graphics systems must be inspected, not guessed at

What to steal:
- GPU instrumentation discipline
- frame inspection discipline
- performance-key naming
- debugging as part of art iteration

Direct implication for VisuLive:
For every major visual pass, capture:
- frame cost by major system
- hot passes
- overdraw suspects
- fill-rate danger zones
- shader / post hotspots

## E. Spatial / immersive world-building references

These references matter because VisuLive needs to stop feeling like a single object in front of a backdrop.
It needs to feel like a **world**.

### teamLab Borderless
Why it matters:
- the core concept is artworks moving out of rooms, relating to other works, influencing each other, and creating one continuous borderless world

What to steal:
- world continuity
- cross-layer interaction
- non-isolated visual events
- the idea that the environment itself is alive and relational

Direct implication for VisuLive:
The chamber should not be static support.
Rings, veils, beams, halos, background stains, and hero residue should influence one another.

### Moment Factory
Why it matters:
- their immersive work merges projection, interactive visuals, lighting, scenography, and sound into transformed spaces rather than isolated screen tricks

What to steal:
- architecture as event partner
- large-form compositional discipline
- room-scale consequence
- treating the environment as a story container

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Design every frame as if the screen is a room opening, not a flat wallpaper behind the hero.

### Refik Anadol
Why it matters:
- his work is useful less as a “copy this style” reference and more as a lesson in scale, data-as-material, and fluid image continuity across large surfaces

What to steal:
- image continuity
- volume illusion
- scale confidence
- the feeling of systems that are alive in time, not just triggered on beats

What not to steal:
- formless AI-slush abstraction
- beautiful but consequence-free drift

Direct implication for VisuLive:
Use continuous image life to support the show, but keep silhouette, event hierarchy, and musical structure strong.

## F. Game-feel and boss-fight staging references

These matter because the best game VFX are readable, authored, and emotionally legible under pressure.

Use these as **principle references**, even when not directly copied:
- Rez Infinite
- Tetris Effect
- Thumper
- Control
- Returnal
- high-end boss fight VFX language in modern action games

What to steal:
- silhouette clarity
- pre-attack tells
- spatial anticipation
- impact states
- aftermath readability
- iconic move sets
- “this attack means something” image language

Direct implication for VisuLive:
A drop should feel more like a boss move than a louder pulse.
A build should feel like charging, tightening, or summoning.
An aftermath should feel like smoke, ghosts, embers, or spatial damage.

# What VisuLive should steal by problem area

## 1. Lighting and glow
Steal from:
- three.js post stack
- Disguise blend/effect logic
- Notch post polish
- Pangolin color tuning discipline

Need to become:
- darker baseline
- hotter events
- more selective bloom
- cleaner emission separation
- less “filled object with bloom on top”
- more “dark mass with electric seams, rims, halos, and event flare”

## 2. Color
Steal from:
- laser systems
- club VJ palettes
- Butterchurn / projectM willingness to transform
- high-end live visuals that commit to a color world

Need to become:
- more stateful
- more daring
- more decisive
- more phrase-aware
- more dangerous when earned

Avoid:
- polite always-blue/cyan safety
- constant rainbow mush

## 3. Environment and chamber
Steal from:
- teamLab’s relational world logic
- Moment Factory’s architectural participation
- laser-show geometry discipline

Need to become:
- a participating world
- not just rings behind an orb
- a place where scale changes are felt
- something that can tighten, widen, rotate, sweep, veil, stain, and reveal

## 4. Framing and composition
Steal from:
- live show widescreen composition
- game boss staging
- projection-mapped environment composition

Need to become:
- less locked-center by habit
- more world-authoritative
- more willing to let hero, chamber, or residue dominate depending on the cue
- more willing to withhold detail for silhouette readability

## 5. Motion and consequence
Steal from:
- grandMA timing and phasers
- TouchDesigner timed processes
- game attack/impact/aftermath rhythm

Need to become:
- more stateful
- more phrase-shaped
- less “constant minor response”
- more “authored motion chapter”

# Curated board plan for the team

Build a **30-item curated reference board** before the next big visual pass.

Do not collect randomly.
Every item must be labeled by function.

## Board categories

### Group 1 — Framing authority (5 items)
Collect examples where the entire screen feels owned, not just the center object.

### Group 2 — Drop consequence (5 items)
Collect examples where impact has scale, staging, and aftermath.

### Group 3 — Laser / beam geometry (4 items)
Collect examples of fan sweeps, tunnels, radial planes, scanning arcs, and precision line worlds.

### Group 4 — Dark-value discipline (4 items)
Collect examples where blacks stay valuable and bright events feel earned.

### Group 5 — Residue / afterimage / stain (4 items)
Collect examples where moments leave ghosts, trails, environmental damage, or light memory.

### Group 6 — Environment as world (4 items)
Collect examples where architecture or environment participates as much as the focal object.

### Group 7 — Hero as emitted structure (4 items)
Collect examples where the focal form reads as a dark anchor wrapped in energy, not a filled glowing toy.

## Labeling format for each item

For every still or clip, record:
- `title`
- `source`
- `link`
- `category`
- `what actually works`
- `what to steal`
- `what to avoid`
- `maps to VisuLive systems`
- `candidate cues or acts`

Example:

```md
### REF-07 — Laser fan cathedral reveal
- Source: Pangolin / live laser show reference
- Category: Laser / beam geometry
- What actually works: radial plane definition + dark negative space + sudden spatial opening
- What to steal: fan ignition grammar, beam spacing discipline, one-direction commitment
- What to avoid: cheesy all-on laser spam
- Maps to VisuLive systems: ChamberSystem, CueEngine, PostResidue
- Candidate cues: cathedral_open, radial_fan_burst, beam_afterglow
```
